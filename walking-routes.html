Walking Routes

and Walking tips

A selection of three routes, showcasing the varied landscape of the Wainwright Fells, to inspire your next walk. Aira Force and Gowbarrow is a short and easy walk that has it all. The Castlerigg and Ashness walk increases the distance while sticking to an easy route. Finally the Coledale Horseshoe is ready to reward you if you can meet it's challenge of distance, steep sections and scrambles.

Aira Force and Gowbarrowa

Difficulty: Easy
Length: 4 mi / 6.5 km
Ascent: 1164 ft / 355 m
Average time: 2 1/2 hours
Start and end point: National trust Aira Force car park

About the walk

A short and easy walk that has it all. Beautiful woodlands, the impressive Aira Force waterfall, numerous smaller waterfalls, a Wainwright peak and views over Ullswater as well as towards the other Eastern fells.

Make the walk your own

Start in Pooley Bridge or Glenridding and catch the Ullswater Steamer. Join in with any National trust trails and activities. Choose a slightly different path route to the one shown here.

Castlerigg and Ashnessa

Including: Walla Crag, Bleaberry Fell and High Seat

Difficulty: Medium
Length: 11.46mi / 18.45km
Ascent: 2470 ft / 753 m
Average time: 6 hours
Start and end point: Keswick

About the walk

Ready to add in some distance, but looking to keep the route easy without any steep bits or scrambles? Then this may be the walk for you. It visits both the ancient Castlerigg Stone Circle and the iconic Ashness Bridge, while boasting spectacular views over both Derwentwater and Thirlmere and off towards the Helvelyn range.

Make the walk your own

Catch the Keswick Launch to take you back to Keswick, while enjoying being on the water and resting your feet. Or shorten the walk by heading straight to Ashness Bridge after summitting Walla Crag.

Coledale Horseshoea

Including: Grisedale Pike, Hopegill Head, Eel Crag/Crag Hill, Sail, Outerside and Barrow

Difficulty: Advanced
Length: 8.66 miles / 14 km
Ascent: 4057 ft / 1237 m
Average time: 5 and a half hours
Start and end point: Braithwaite

About the walk

Ready to add in some height, some steep sections and some scrambles? If you are, then this walk will reward you with experiencing the impressive Hobcarton Crag and spectacular views over the North Western fells, back towards Derwentwater and Keswick and even across to the Solway Firth and Scotland on a clear day.

Make the walk your own

Shorten the walk by descending back down after Hopegill Head via Force Crag Mine and along Coledale Beck back to Braithwaite. Or, add in other Wainwrights such as Whiteside, Grasmoor, Wandope, Whiteless Peak, Scar Crags or Causey Pike.

Walking tips a

Walking in the Lake District is a wonderful activity and is relatively safe. Hiking does, however, carry a risk of injury or worse. You should be aware of, and accept these risks, and be responsible for your actions.

The following tips will help you to stay safe and make your walk more enjoyable:

  • Plan your route in advance. Make sure you know what landscapes you will encounter (steep, rocky, cliff edge, boggy), features to look for to ensure you stay on route, and how long you anticipate the walk to take (make sure to leave plenty of time). Plan alternative routes should you become tired or short on time.

  • Check the weather. Weatherline is a website that provides an up to date weather forecast specific to walking in the Lake District. Check the forecast the day before you set off and again on the day of your walk. Conditions can change dramatically even in a short space of time. Avoid steep and exposed ground if storms and /or high winds are forecast. High rainfall can result in rivers becoming impassable and rocky ground slippery underfoot. All extremes of temperature can be hazardous. Always be prepared to turn back.

  • Inform others. Tell someone where you are going and leave details of the route of your planned walk and your estimated time of completion. Remember to let them know once you are safely back.

  • Take enough bottled water with you to stay properly hydrated, especially in warm weather.

  • Take enough food for the day. Look for foods that are high in carbohydrates for energy.

  • Take clothing adequate for all weather. It might look like a lovely sunny day from your hotel or campsite, but the tops of the fells can paint an entirely different picture, and the weather can change very quickly thanks to the prevailing winds that come from the Atlantic Ocean.

  • Take a map and compass and the ability to read it. Do not rely on a map on a phone or tablet alone, as GPS can be unreliable.

  • Take a mobile phone, which you can use to contact Mountain Rescue (call 999 and ask for Cumbria Police) in an emergency. Make sure you preserve your battery so that your phone is there when you need it. You should call 999:

    • If you or a member of your party sustains an injury warranting the assistance of a mountain rescue team.
    • If you come across someone who has sustained an injury warranting the assistance of a mountain rescue team.
    • If one of your party has become separated from the main party and you have made every effort to but have been unable to meet up again.

  • Pack a torch (Including spare batteries). If case you get caught after dark.

  • Pack a whistle which will help you to alert MRTs and other walkers of your presence and need for assistance if required.

  • Pack a first aid kit (there are specific walking first aid kits that you can buy)
